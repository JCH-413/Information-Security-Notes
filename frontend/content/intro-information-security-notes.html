Information Security — Course Introduction

📖 Lecture Overview

This opening lecture lays the foundation for the whole course. It maps the areas of information security, defines core concepts (information, security, information security), states the security objectives and the wider ITU-T X.800 security services, introduces the RMIAS reference model, distinguishes active vs. passive attacks, and establishes the standard terminology (asset, vulnerability, threat, risk, countermeasure, policy).

🌐 Why It Matters Today

Security figures prominently in the 21st-century world, spanning personal security and information security (protecting the electronic information of organizations and users).

  • There is no simple solution; there are many different types of attacks, and defending against them is often difficult.
  • Information security continues to rank as the number one concern of IT managers.
  • Despite tens of billions of dollars spent annually, the number of successful attacks keeps increasing.

📰 Today's Security Attacks (Examples)

  • Fake anti-virus
  • Taking control of wireless cameras
  • ATM machine attacks (malware called Ploutus)
  • Taking over Twitter accounts
  • Attackers using online sites such as Craigslist and eBay to lure victims into downloading malware

Defining Information, Security & InfoSec

📄 What is Information?

Something meaningful, conveyed by a sequence of symbols.

  • Symbols can be alphabetic characters, numbers, punctuation, etc.
  • It can be physical or logical — a book, or something on a computer.
  • It is accurate, timely, contextualised, relevant, and purposeful — specific and able to be organized.

🔒 What is Security?

The state of being free from danger or threats, applicable to both physical and logical scenarios. The Cambridge English Dictionary defines security as the protection of a building, a person, an organization, or a country against threats such as crime.

Security can be provided through:

  • Physical artifacts such as walls and locks.
  • Logical means — people and processes, inspections, surveillance, authorizations (as found in airports).

🛡️ What is Information Security?

The practice of defending information from un-authorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, inspection, recording, or destruction.

  • A computing-based discipline involving technology, people, information, and processes to enable secured operations of an organization.
  • It involves the creation, operation, analysis, and testing of secure computer systems.
  • It is interdisciplinary, including law, policy, human factors, ethics, and risk management in the context of adversaries.
04

Security, Safety, Privacy & Trade-offs

Security

Protection against intentional, human-made threats (malicious acts).

Threat source: attackers, hackers, insiders, criminals.

Safety

Protection against unintentional, accidental, or environmental harm.

Threat source: accidents, natural disasters, system failures, human error.

Privacy (Person-Centered)

The right of individuals to control how their personal data is collected, used, and shared.

🖥️ NIST Definition of Computer Security

The NIST Computer Security Handbook defines computer security as the protection afforded to an automated information system in order to attain the applicable objectives of preserving the integrity, availability, and confidentiality of information-system resources.

Resources include hardware, software, firmware, information/data, and telecommunications.

⚖️ Understanding Security

Security is both the goal to be free from danger and the process that achieves that freedom. Harm may come from two sources:

  • A direct action intended to inflict damage.
  • An indirect and unintentional action.
As security is increased, convenience is often decreased — security is often described as sacrificing convenience for safety. The more secure something is, the less convenient it may be to use.

Security Objectives — The CIA Triad

Confidentiality

Assures that private or confidential information is not made available or disclosed to unauthorized individuals.

Integrity

Assures that information and programs are changed only in a specified and authorized manner.

Availability

Assures that systems work promptly and service is not denied to authorized users.

💳 Quiz — Confidentiality of a Credit Card

Suppose you make an online purchase and pay by credit card. What is the best way to preserve confidentiality of your card information?

HTTPS (SSL/TLS) + trusted merchant + PayPal/virtual card
+ private network + transaction alerts
= strongest confidentiality

🧩 Integrity — Breaches & Countermeasures

Events that lead to integrity breaches (often non-intentional): accidentally deleting files, entering invalid data, altering configurations.

Countermeasures:

  • Hashing (data integrity)
  • Configuration management (system integrity)
  • Access control (physical and technical)
  • Software digital signing
  • Transmission cyclic redundancy check (CRC) functions

⚙️ Availability — Countermeasures

  • Redundant Array of Independent Disks (RAID)
  • Load balancing
  • Redundant data and power lines
  • Software and data backups
  • Co-location and offsite facilities
  • Rollback functions
  • Failover configurations

Case Study — A Classic Data Breach

🎣 How the Breach Unfolds

  • An employee is sent a phishing email with a link to a realistic-looking internal site.
  • The employee opens the email, clicks the link, and types their username and password.
  • The malicious site collects the password and shows the user that everything is fine, so they are not suspicious.
  • The malicious actor uses the username and password to download sensitive files.
Phishing email
→
Credentials entered
→
Password harvested
→
Sensitive files stolen

🛡️ Defense in Three Stages

  • Prevention: detect phishing URLs and mark as spam, train employees to notice phishing, identify and block offsite access of sensitive files, encrypt files so they are useless if leaked.
  • Detection: identify that sensitive files have been accessed from offsite.
  • Response: change the employee's password, notify the CTO, notify the insurer, begin the post-breach plan.

Security Services — ITU-T (X.800)

📋 The Six Services

X.800 extends the CIA triad into six security services: Confidentiality, Authentication, Integrity, Non-repudiation, Availability, and Access Control. Each counters a specific class of attack.

ServicePurposeAssociated Attack
Confidentiality (Secrecy)Protect transmitted data; protect against traffic analysis; timelinessInterception — unauthorised party gains access to data
AuthenticationAssurance the message is from the proper source; mutual authenticationFabrication — insertion of counterfeit messages / masquerade
IntegrityMessage received as sent; also covers replay, re-ordering, deletion, delayModification — gains access and tampers with messages
Non-repudiationPrevents parties from denying they sent/received a message; sender and receiver can prove the other partyRepudiation attempt — party falsely claims keys/messages were stolen
AvailabilityAvailability of information/resourcesInterruption (cut the cable) / Denial of Service (noisy comms)
Access ControlLimit & control access to hosts, services, networks; authenticate each party; fine-grained DRM; auditing serviceReplay — record a legitimate message (e.g., a login) and replay later

🔍 Non-repudiation in Detail

Concerned with protecting against legitimate protocol participants, not an external source. The receiver can verify and prove who sent a message, and the sender can verify and prove who received it.

RMIAS — Reference Model for Information Assurance & Security

🧭 What RMIAS Captures

RMIAS organizes information security across four dimensions: an Information Taxonomy, a set of Security Goals, a Security Development Life Cycle, and a catalogue of Security Countermeasures.

📚 Information Taxonomy

Information is classified along several axes:

  • Sensitivity — how sensitive the information is.
  • Location — where the information resides.
  • Form — Paper, Electronic, Verbal.
  • State — Creation, Storage, Processing, Transmission, Destruction.

🎯 Security Goals (Eight Petals)

RMIAS expands the goals beyond CIA to eight: Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability, Privacy, Non-repudiation, Auditability, Authenticity & Trustworthiness, and Accountability.

🧰 Security Countermeasures (Four Categories)

  • Organisational — Strategy, Audit, Policy, Procedures, Governance.
  • Technical — Cryptography, Authentication, Authorisation.
  • Human-Oriented — Training, Culture, Education, Ethics, Motivation.
  • Legal — Law, Contracts, Agreements.

Active vs. Passive Attacks

📖 Classification (X.800 & RFC 4949)

Security attacks are classified as passive or active:

  • A passive attack attempts to learn or make use of information from the system but does not affect system resources.
  • An active attack attempts to alter system resources or affect their operation.

🕵️ Passive Attacks

In the nature of eavesdropping on or monitoring transmissions; the opponent's goal is to obtain the information being transmitted. Two types:

  • Release of message contents
  • Traffic analysis

Hard to detect (no alteration); emphasis is on prevention.

⚔️ Active Attacks

Involve modification of the data stream or creation of a false stream. Difficult to prevent due to the wide variety of vulnerabilities; the goal is to detect and recover. Four kinds:

  • Masquerade — pretending to be a legitimate user/system to gain unauthorized access by leveraging trust.
  • Replay — passive capture of a data unit and its later retransmission to produce an unauthorized effect.
  • Modification of messages — altering, delaying, or reordering part of a legitimate message.
  • Denial of Service — prevents or inhibits the normal use/management of communications facilities.

Core Security Terminology

💎 Asset

An item of value — data in an information system; a service provided by a system; a system capability; or an item of system equipment (a component — hardware, firmware, software, or documentation).

🔑 Vulnerability, Threat, Threat Agent, Attack

  • Vulnerability: a flaw or weakness in a system's design, implementation, or operation/management that could be exploited to violate the system's security policy.
  • Threat: a potential for violation of security — a circumstance, capability, action, or event that could breach security and cause harm. A possible danger that might exploit a vulnerability.
  • Threat agent: a person or element with the power to carry out a threat.
  • Attack: a threat that is carried out (a threat action).

📊 Risk, Countermeasure, Security Policy

  • Risk: an expectation of loss expressed as the probability that a particular threat will exploit a particular vulnerability with a particular harmful result.
  • Countermeasure: an action, device, procedure, or technique that reduces a threat, vulnerability, or attack by eliminating/preventing it, minimizing its harm, or discovering and reporting it so corrective action can be taken.
  • Security Policy: a set of rules and practices that specify or regulate how a system or organization provides security services to protect sensitive and critical resources.
Risk = P(threat exploits vulnerability) × harmful result risk combines likelihood with impact

General Attacks, Assets, Threats & Countermeasures

🎯 General Attacks That Threaten Security Goals

  • Eavesdropping — learning confidential information.
  • Modification — modifying a message in transit.
  • Denial of service — making the service unavailable (e.g., by keeping it busy).
  • Forging — building a fake message, pretending it was sent by someone else.
  • Masquerade — posing as someone else in a single message or interactive protocol.
  • Repudiation — denying having sent or received some message.
  • Profiling — gathering information about a single user.
  • Fingerprinting — identifying the user associated with some message.
  • Traffic analysis — learning origin, destination, length, and times of communications (not the content).

💼 Assets

Tangible and intangible items of value, including: data and archives; computers, disks, tapes; CPU time, storage, network capacity; comms equipment (routers, switches, firewalls, modems, bandwidth), phones, faxes; air-conditioning/alarm systems and physical security; manuals and guides; printouts (reports, letters, emails, contracts); configuration information; passwords; staff; safety and health of personnel; privacy of users; public image and reputation; customer goodwill; share price; intellectual property; domain name.

⚠️ Threats

Hardware errors; terrorists; theft and malicious acts; industrial/government spying; malicious software (viruses, worms, trojan horses, bombs, trap doors, spoofs, password crackers, cryptanalysis); pirating; password cracking; denial of service; masquerade; misuse of resources; social engineering; reverse engineering; acts of God (fire, earthquakes); disaffected employees; human error; illness & injury; economic downturns; hackers; government/military/industrial spies; criminals; ISPs and backbone providers.

The slide poses the question: what is the biggest threat? — often the insider/human element.

🧰 Countermeasures

Protect buildings, equipment, and people from unauthorised access and natural disasters; use fibre-optic cabling and shield equipment/cabling; use reliable hardware & software and shredders; keep backups and standby systems; use good cryptography; use firewalls and simulated attacks; use good password admin, virus checkers, intrusion-detection and auditing software, and biometrics; isolate the network; employ counter-intelligence, ethical hackers, security guards, lawyers; employ trustworthy staff with background checks; train/educate staff; keep staff happy; insure; keep good legal backup; and take security seriously (planning, administration, risk assessment, cost/benefit analysis).

📜 Policies

A set of well-defined, consistent, and implementable rules (security requirements). Policies should be general and change little over time. Example exercise: for an online auction company like eBay (where users buy and sell goods, sellers post listings, and buyers bid), what policies might the users want applied?

Risk Management & the Network Access Model

📉 Five Options to Deal with Risk

  • Risk avoidance — identify the risk but do not engage in the activity (don't buy the scooter).
  • Acceptance — acknowledge the risk but take no steps to address it (ignore it and buy the scooter anyway).
  • Risk mitigation — address the risk to make it less serious (ask management to fix the fence).
  • Deterrence — understand the attacker and inform them of the consequences (put up a warning sign).
  • Transference — transfer the risk to a third party (insurance).

🚪 Network Access Security Model

An opponent — human (e.g., a hacker) or software (e.g., a virus or worm) — attempts to reach an information system through an access channel. A gatekeeper function stands in the way, and internal security controls protect the resources within.

  • The information system holds computing resources (processor, memory, I/O), data, processes, and software.
  • The gatekeeper authenticates and filters access; internal controls provide a second layer of defense.

Why InfoSec Is Challenging

🧩 The Difficulties

  • Security is not simple.
  • Potential attacks on the security features themselves must be considered.
  • Procedures used to provide services are often counter-intuitive.
  • It is necessary to decide where to use the various security mechanisms.
  • Requires constant monitoring, and is too often an afterthought.
  • Security mechanisms typically involve more than a single algorithm or protocol.
  • Security is essentially a battle of wits between a perpetrator and the designer.
  • Little benefit from security investment is perceived until a failure occurs.
  • Strong security is often viewed as an impediment to efficient and user-friendly operation.
