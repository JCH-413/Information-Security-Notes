01

Block Ciphers & What DES Is

📖 Symmetric Block Encryption Algorithms

A block cipher processes the plaintext input in fixed-size blocks and produces a block of ciphertext of equal size for each plaintext block. The same secret key is used for both encryption and decryption (symmetric).

  • DES — Data Encryption Standard (the focus of this lecture)
  • Triple DES (3DES)
  • AES, etc.

🏛️ The Data Encryption Standard (DES)

  • Designed by IBM.
  • It is a block cipher.
  • Adopted in 1977 by the National Bureau of Standards (NBS) — now the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) — as Federal Information Processing Standard (FIPS) 46.
  • It was unbroken for more than 10 years after publication. Some aspects of its design were kept secret by IBM at the request of the US National Security Agency (NSA).
  • Some people believed IBM and NSA had hidden a trapdoor in DES that only they knew about (which they could use to crack DES).

⚠️ DES Criticism (before adoption)

Before being adopted as a standard, DES drew intense criticism on two fronts:

  • First — an enormous reduction in the key, leaving it too short to withstand a brute-force attack.
  • Second — the internal structure of the S-boxes was classified (kept secret).

📈 DES Wide Use

  • DES flourished in financial applications.
  • In 1994, NIST reaffirmed its use for another 5 years.
  • In 1999, NIST issued a new version of its standard — Triple DES.

🔁 High-Level Overview

DES encryption takes a 64-bit plaintext (P.T) and a 56-bit key to produce a 64-bit ciphertext (C.T). Decryption is the reverse cipher using the same key:

General Structure of DES

📖 The Three-Stage Pipeline

The full DES encryption has three phases applied to the 64-bit plaintext, while the 56-bit cipher key feeds a separate round-key generator:

  1. Initial Permutation (IP) — rearranges the 64 input bits once.
  2. 16 rounds — each a Feistel round driven by its own 48-bit subkey (K1 … K16).
  3. Final Permutation (FP / IP⁻¹) — the inverse of IP, applied once at the end.

The overall data path looks like this — plaintext enters at the top, each round mixes in a 48-bit subkey from the key generator on the right, and the final permutation yields the ciphertext:

🗺️ DES General Structure (diagram)

Reading top-to-bottom on the left, and right-to-left for the keys:

  • 64-bit P.T → Initial Permutation → Round 1 (fed by K1, 48-bit) → … (dashed) … → Round 16 (fed by K16, 48-bit) → Final Permutation → 64-bit C.T.
  • On the right, the Round-key generator takes the 56-bit cipher key and produces each round's 48-bit subkey.

🔧 Initial & Final Permutation

  • Initial Permutation (IP) — occurs only once, before the first round.
  • Final Permutation (FP) — occurs once, after the last round.
  • Both IP and FP are straight P-boxes that are inverses of each other. They are predefined tables and have no cryptographic significance in DES (they don't add security; they're a hardware-era artifact).
Analogy — A Feistel Round

Think of each round like shuffling a two-handed deck. You keep the left half aside, scramble the right half with a secret recipe (the round function and subkey), then mix that scramble into the left half by XOR. Finally you swap hands so the scrambled side becomes the side you keep next time. Because the swap and XOR are reversible, the very same machine run backwards undoes everything — which is why decryption reuses the encryption circuitry.

Initial & Final Permutation (with the real tables)

📖 Permutation Concept

A permutation simply rearranges the bits of a block — no bit is added or removed in a straight permutation. The mapping is fixed and public (a "predefined table"). IP runs before the 16 rounds; FP runs after them, and FP is exactly the inverse of IP.

Reading a permutation table: the table lists, for each output position, which input bit goes there. So in IP, output bit 1 = input bit 58, output bit 2 = input bit 50, and so on.

04

Inside a Single Round & the DES Function f

📖 The Single Round (Feistel)

Each 64-bit value entering a round is treated as two 32-bit halves: L (left) and R (right). The round key Kᵢ is 48 bits; the R input is 32 bits.

  • The R input is first expanded to 48 bits using a table that duplicates 16 of the R bits.
  • The resulting 48 bits are XORed with Kᵢ.
  • This 48-bit result passes through the substitution function (S-boxes), producing a 32-bit output.
  • That 32-bit output is then permuted (the straight P-box).

⚙️ The Feistel Round Equations

Li = Ri-1
Ri = Li-1 ⊕ f(Ri-1, Ki)
the left half just copies the previous right half; the new right half is the old left XOR the round function

Inside the function f(R, K), the order of operations is:

R (32)
→
Expansion P-Box (48)
→
XOR Kᵢ (Whitener)
→
8 S-Boxes (32)
→
Straight P-Box (32)

The four components of the DES function are therefore: Expansion P-Box → Whitener (XOR with key) → Group of S-Boxes → Straight P-Box.

Expansion, S-Boxes & the Straight P-Box

📖 Expansion Permutation (E)

The expansion permutation acts on the 32-bit input to the cipher function. It expands 32 bits to 48 bits by duplicating some input bits at specified positions. (This is what lets a 32-bit half be XORed against a 48-bit subkey.)

📖 Substitution Boxes (S-Boxes)

The S-boxes are the heart of DES security — the only non-linear step.

  • Each S-box maps a 6-bit input block to a 4-bit output block.
  • There are 8 S-boxes, so the 48-bit input (8 × 6) is mapped to a 32-bit output (8 × 4).

How the 6 bits address the table:

row = bit₁ bit₆ (the outer/first & sixth bits → 2-bit row index, 0–3)
column = bit₂ bit₃ bit₄ bit₅ (the middle four bits → 4-bit column index, 0–15)

06

Key Generation (The Key Schedule)

📖 Round-Key Generator

The round-key generator creates sixteen 48-bit subkeys out of a 56-bit cipher key. The actual key supplied is 64 bits, from which 8 parity bits are dropped.

  • Parity Drop positions (the bits removed): 8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, 56, 64.
  • The schedule uses two fixed permutation tables — PC1 (Permuted Choice One) and PC2 (Permuted Choice Two) — and a table of shifts (LS).

🔄 The Shift Schedule (LS)

At each round the two 28-bit halves are circularly left-shifted (rotated):

Rounds 1, 2, 9, 16 → shift by 1 bit
All other rounds → shift by 2 bits
total shifts across 16 rounds = 28, which returns C/D to start

⚙️ Key Schedule — Step by Step

  1. The 64-bit key is the input. It is first subjected to permutation PC1 (Permuted Choice One), which also drops the parity bits.
  2. The resulting 56-bit key is treated as two 28-bit halves, C₀ and D₀.
  3. At each round, Cᵢ₋₁ and Dᵢ₋₁ are separately circularly left-shifted (1 or 2 bits per the LS table). These shifted values feed the next round.
  4. They also feed PC2 (Permuted Choice Two), which produces the 48-bit output subkey Kᵢ used inside the round function f(Rᵢ₋₁, Kᵢ).

The permutation function is the same each round, but a different subkey results because of the accumulated repeated shifts of the key bits.

The control flow of the schedule — generate one 48-bit subkey per round, loop until round 16:

Step 1

Key (64) → PC1 → Effective Key (56)

Drop the 8 parity bits and permute via PC1, giving the 56-bit effective key, split into C₀ and D₀ (28 bits each).

Step 2

Set round number = 1

Begin the loop that will produce K1 … K16.

Step 3

Circular left shift (LS) of both halves

Shift Cᵢ₋₁ and Dᵢ₋₁ independently by 1 or 2 bits according to the round number.

Step 4

PC2 → Subkey (48)

Apply Permuted Choice Two to the joined 56 bits to select the 48-bit subkey Kᵢ.

Step 5

Is round number = 16?

If No → increment the round number and loop back to Step 3. If Yes → End; all 16 subkeys are ready.

07

Full Encryption & Decryption Flow

📖 DES Encryption — the data side

There are two inputs to the encryption function: plaintext and key. The plaintext must be 64 bits and the key is 56 bits effective. Processing the plaintext proceeds in these phases:

  1. The 64-bit plaintext passes through an initial permutation (IP) that rearranges the bits to produce the permuted input.
  2. This is followed by 16 rounds of the same function, involving substitution and permutation.
  3. The output of the 16th round is 64 bits that are a function of the input plaintext and the key.
  4. The left and right halves of that output are swapped to produce the preoutput.
  5. Finally the preoutput passes through IP⁻¹ (the final permutation) to produce the 64-bit ciphertext.

🔑 DES Encryption — the key side

The right-hand portion of the schematic shows how the 56-bit key is used:

  • Initially the key is passed through a permutation function (PC1).
  • For each of the 16 rounds, a subkey Kᵢ is produced by a combination of a left circular shift and a permutation (PC2).
  • The permutation function is the same each round, but a different subkey is produced because of the repeated shifts of the key bits.

🔓 DES Decryption

Decryption uses the same algorithm as encryption — except the subkeys are applied in reverse order (K16 first, down to K1). This is the elegant payoff of the Feistel structure: one circuit does both directions.

Encrypt: apply K1, K2, …, K16
Decrypt: apply K16, K15, …, K1

The Strength & Breaking of DES

💪 The Strength of DES (key space)

  • The key length is 56 bits, so there are 2⁵⁶ possible keys ≈ 7.2 × 10¹⁶ keys — enough that a naïve brute-force attack was once impractical.
  • Assuming on average half the key space must be searched, a single machine doing one DES encryption per microsecond would take more than 1000 years to break the cipher.

📜 The Diffie–Hellman Prediction (1977)

In 1977, Diffie & Hellman postulated that the technology existed to build a parallel machine with 1 million encryption devices, each performing one encryption per microsecond.

search time ≈ 10 hours
estimated cost ≈ $20 million

💥 DES Proved Insecure (1998)

DES was finally proved insecure in July 1998, when the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) declared DES broken using a purpose-built "DES cracker" costing about $250,000.

The EFF published the description of the cracker, enabling others to build their own. With the steady decrease in hardware price and increase in speed, this made plain DES worthless for serious security — which is why the field moved to Triple DES and then AES.

📝 Common Exam Questions
  • How many possible DES keys are there? → 2⁵⁶ ≈ 7.2 × 10¹⁶.
  • Why was the key criticized? → 56 bits is too short to withstand brute force.
  • Who broke DES and when? → The EFF, July 1998, with a ~$250,000 "DES cracker".
  • What replaced DES? → Triple DES (1999), then AES.
