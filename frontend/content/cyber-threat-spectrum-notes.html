The Cyber Threat Spectrum — Introduction

📖 Lecture Overview

This lecture surveys the full spectrum of cyber threats and how to defend against them. It covers the principles of secure design, the major security frameworks and standards (NIST, ISO/IEC 27001, GDPR, CIS), the threat posed by employees and ex-employees, the criminal era of competitor threats (corporate espionage), and finally cyberwar and cyberterrorism.

02

Fundamental Security Design Principles

📖 Source

The National Centers of Academic Excellence in Information Assurance/Cyber Defense — jointly sponsored by the U.S. National Security Agency and the U.S. Department of Homeland Security — list the following fundamental security design principles [NCAE13].

🔑 The Principles

  • Economy of mechanism — keep security designs simple and small to minimize vulnerabilities.
  • Fail-safe defaults — deny access by default unless explicitly permitted.
  • Complete mediation — check every access request against security policies without exception.
  • Open design — security should not rely on secrecy of design or implementation.
  • Separation of privilege — require multiple independent conditions for access.
  • Least privilege — grant only the minimum access rights necessary to perform a task.
  • Least common mechanism — limit sharing of resources to reduce interference and information leaks.
  • Psychological acceptability — design controls that are easy for users to understand and use.
  • Isolation — separate system components to contain faults and limit damage.
  • Encapsulation — hide internal system details behind well-defined interfaces.
  • Modularity — build systems with interchangeable and independent modules.
  • Layering — use multiple defensive layers to protect against failures.
  • Least astonishment — ensure system behavior matches user expectations to avoid errors.
03

Security Frameworks & Standards

📖 What They Are

Cybersecurity standards are published methods to protect users and organizations online. They cover users, networks, devices, software, processes, data (stored or moving), applications, services, and connected systems.

🎯 Principal Objective & Contents

The principal objective is to reduce risks, including preventing or mitigating cyber-attacks. Published materials include collections of:

  • Tools
  • Policies
  • Security concepts and safeguards
  • Guidelines and risk management approaches
  • Actions and training
  • Best practices, assurance, and technologies

🧭 Understanding Cybersecurity Frameworks

Frameworks are comprehensive sets of guidelines, standards, and best practices aimed at managing cyber risks effectively. They serve as strategic tools to minimize an organization's vulnerabilities and reduce the chances of exploitation by hackers and cybercriminals.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

📖 What NIST CSF Provides

The NIST CSF offers detailed cybersecurity guidance for private-sector organizations in the US. It provides a high-level classification of cybersecurity outcomes and a method to assess and manage them, and is adaptable to organizations of various sizes and maturity levels.

⚙️ The Six Functions (Addressed Concurrently)

The functions should be addressed concurrently:

  • GOVERN, IDENTIFY, PROTECT, and DETECT — supporting actions should happen continuously.
  • RESPOND and RECOVER — should be ready at all times and happen when cybersecurity incidents occur.

All functions have vital roles: GOVERN, IDENTIFY, and PROTECT outcomes help prevent and prepare for incidents, while GOVERN, DETECT, RESPOND, and RECOVER outcomes help discover and manage incidents.

📋 Five Steps to Create & Use a CSF Organizational Profile

  1. Scope the Organizational Profile
  2. Gather needed information
  3. Create the Organizational Profile
  4. Analyze gaps and create an action plan
  5. Implement the action plan and update the Profile

…then repeat — it is a continuous cycle.

ISO/IEC 27001

📖 The ISMS Standard

ISO/IEC 27001 is part of the ISO/IEC 27000 series/family — an Information Security Management System (ISMS) standard.

  • The latest version (referenced here) was published in October 2013 by ISO and IEC.
  • Full title: ISO/IEC 27001:2013 — Information technology — Security techniques — Information security management systems — Requirements.
  • It formally specifies a management system to control information security explicitly.

Reference book: Principles of Information Security.

06

GDPR — General Data Protection Regulation

📖 Overview

The GDPR is a comprehensive legal framework established by the European Union to safeguard the privacy and security of personal data belonging to its residents.

  • Introduced in 2016.
  • Applies to any organization that handles the personal information of EU citizens, no matter where the organization is based globally.

✅ GDPR Compliance Checklist

  • Data Mapping — document what personal data is collected, why, and how it's processed/stored.
  • Legal Basis — identify the lawful reason for each data-processing activity.
  • Policies — create clear, plain-language privacy policies and consent forms.
  • Training — educate staff on GDPR and data-protection basics.
  • Third-Party Management — ensure vendors follow GDPR and have proper contracts.
  • Audits — regularly review compliance and fix gaps.
  • DPO — appoint a Data Protection Officer if required.
  • DPIAs — assess risks for high-impact data projects.
  • Security Measures — use encryption, MFA, and regular security checks.
  • Data Rights — enable access, correction, deletion, and objection.
  • Breach Response — have a plan to detect and report data breaches fast.

CIS Controls

📖 Center for Internet Security (CIS)

  • A prioritized set of cybersecurity best practices.
  • Originally known as SANS Top 20; now managed by CIS.
  • Globally recognized for reducing risk from common threats.
  • Suitable for organizations of all sizes and sectors.

1. Basic Controls (CIS 1–5)

Establish core cybersecurity hygiene.

  • CIS 1: Inventory of hardware assets
  • CIS 2: Inventory of software assets
  • CIS 3: Data protection (encryption, DLP)
  • CIS 4: Secure configuration of systems
  • CIS 5: Account lifecycle management

2. Foundational Controls (CIS 6–10)

Strengthen defenses and monitoring.

  • CIS 6: Access control (least privilege)
  • CIS 7: Continuous vulnerability management
  • CIS 8: Audit log collection and analysis
  • CIS 9: Email & browser protections
  • CIS 10: Malware defenses

3. Organizational Controls (CIS 11–15)

Embed security into governance and culture.

  • CIS 11: Data recovery capabilities
  • CIS 12: Secure network infrastructure
  • CIS 13: Security awareness training
  • CIS 14: Secure software development
  • CIS 15: Third-party/service provider management

💡 Why Use CIS Controls?

  • Actionable, prioritized, and cost-effective
  • Protects against real-world threats
  • Useful for both new and mature security programs
  • Supports compliance and risk management

Employee & Ex-Employee Threats

⚠️ Why Insiders Matter

  • Employees and ex-employees are a major source of computer crimes.
  • Many perpetrators had legitimate or recent access to systems.
  • Managing personnel with access is a critical security priority.

🤷 Unintentional Threats

  • Forgetting security rules
  • Failing to follow procedures
  • Creating vulnerabilities unknowingly

😈 Intentional Threats

  • Knowingly violating controls
  • Assisting in security breaches

📋 Examples of Internal Threats

  • Unauthorized access or enabling others
  • Altering or deleting data
  • Crashing or destroying systems
  • Misusing systems for personal gain
  • Holding data hostage
  • Stealing customer/strategic data (espionage, fraud)
09

Personnel Security — Hiring to Termination

🔍 Background Checks & Screening

  • Resume verification (employment & education history)
  • Interview discrepancies with experienced staff
  • Intensive checks for sensitive roles: background investigation agency, criminal record check, credit record check (financial-stress risks), polygraph (where legal), and bonding for sensitive positions

📝 Employment Agreements

Contracts should include:

  • Confidentiality & non-disclosure agreements
  • Employee obligations to protect information
  • Reference to the organization's security policy

Employees must review and agree to abide by the policy.

🛡️ Security During Employment

  • Ongoing awareness and training programs
  • Policies for email & Internet use (e.g., NUCES examples)
  • Key principles: Least privilege (minimal access required), Separation of duties (avoid conflicts of interest), and avoid over-reliance on key employees → build redundancy

🚪 Termination of Employment (ISO 27002)

Objective: an orderly exit & removal of access. Key actions:

  • Remove from access lists
  • Inform guards/security teams
  • Revoke personal access codes/cards
  • Change locks / reprogram systems
  • Recover company assets (IDs, devices, documents)
  • Notify relevant departments
Security measures must cover the entire lifecycle: Hiring → Employment → Termination. Strong personnel security reduces the risks of insider misuse, espionage, fraud, and sabotage.

Competitor Threats & Corporate Espionage

🏢 The Criminal Era

Businesses face threats not only from hackers but also from competitors, who may use unethical or illegal tactics to gain advantage — known as corporate espionage or industrial spying.

🎯 Nature of Competitor Threats

  • Theft of trade secrets and intellectual property
  • Unauthorized access to confidential business data
  • Manipulation of systems to cause financial or reputational harm
  • Targeting strategic assets (customers, designs, formulas)

🛠️ Common Techniques

  • Hacking into rival networks
  • Planting insiders or bribing employees
  • Stealing customer or supplier databases
  • Reverse engineering of products
  • Spreading misinformation or fake news

💰 Motivations

  • Gaining market advantage
  • Disrupting rival operations
  • Copying innovations without R&D costs
  • Winning contracts through stolen insights

📚 Real-World Examples

Technology, pharmaceutical, and defense industries are prime targets; state-sponsored competitors may also engage in cyber-theft.

  • Coca-Cola vs. Pepsi (2006) — Pepsi employees caught attempting to steal Coke's trade secrets.
  • Google vs. Uber (2017) — lawsuit over stolen self-driving car technology.
  • DuPont (2011) — a Chinese company convicted of stealing the chemical formula for Kevlar.

📉 Consequences for Victims

  • Financial losses (millions to billions)
  • Loss of customer trust and loyalty
  • Competitive disadvantage in the market
  • Legal and regulatory consequences

🛡️ Defense Strategies & ISO 27002 Perspective

  • Strong cybersecurity policies & monitoring
  • Employee awareness and training (reduce insider risk)
  • Use of encryption & secure communication
  • Regular audits and access-control reviews
  • Legal action & collaboration with authorities

ISO 27002 security objectives: protect intellectual property, prevent misuse of sensitive information, and ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of corporate data.

Cyberwar & Cyberterrorism

⚔️ Cyberwar

The use of computer technology to disrupt the activities of a state or organization, especially the deliberate attacking of information systems for strategic or military purposes.

💥 Cyberterrorism

Hacking to conduct terrorist activities via networks or the Internet.

🏛️ Government Focus

Governments worldwide are working to secure critical computing systems, communication networks, and physical & power infrastructures.

📜 Origin & FBI Definition

  • Origin: the term was coined in the 1980s by Barry Collin (Institute for Security and Intelligence, California), defined as the convergence of cyberspace + terrorism.
  • FBI (Mark Pollitt) defines cyberterrorism as premeditated, politically motivated attacks targeting information, computer systems, programs, and data, resulting in violence against noncombatant targets, carried out by subnational groups or clandestine agents.

🕰️ Early Instances

  • Defacement of NATO websites during the Kosovo War (1998–1999).
  • Some experts considered cyberterrorism to be hype that distracts from more pressing cybersecurity issues.

🌐 Notable Attack — DNS Root Servers (Oct 21, 2002)

  • A Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attack targeted the 13 root DNS servers.
  • 9 out of 13 servers were jammed.
  • The problem was mitigated quickly; most Internet users were unaffected.
  • At peak, only 6% of DNS requests went unanswered (normally DNS responds almost 100% of the time).

Though limited, the attack showed the potential scale of cyberterrorism threats.

Cyberterrorism poses risks to national security and critical infrastructure. Even small disruptions highlight vulnerabilities in global systems, so continuous improvement in cyber defense strategies is essential.

  • Name the six NIST CSF functions and say which prevent vs. manage incidents. → Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover; Govern/Identify/Protect prevent & prepare, Govern/Detect/Respond/Recover discover & manage
  • What are the five steps to create and use a CSF Organizational Profile? → Scope, gather information, create the profile, analyze gaps/create an action plan, implement and update (repeat)
  • What does ISO/IEC 27001 specify? → A formal Information Security Management System (ISMS) to control information security
  • What is GDPR and to whom does it apply? → An EU data-protection regulation (2016) applying to any organization handling EU citizens' personal data, regardless of location
  • Describe the three tiers of CIS Controls. → Basic (1–5) core hygiene, Foundational (6–10) defenses/monitoring, Organizational (11–15) governance/culture
  • Differentiate unintentional and intentional insider threats with examples. → Unintentional: forgetting rules, not following procedures; Intentional: knowingly violating controls, assisting breaches
  • What personnel-security measures span the employment lifecycle? → Background checks/screening at hiring, NDAs and training during employment, and ISO 27002 orderly access removal at termination
  • What is corporate espionage, and name common techniques and a real case. → Competitors stealing trade secrets/IP via hacking, insiders/bribery, DB theft, reverse engineering, misinformation; e.g., Coke/Pepsi (2006), Google/Uber (2017), DuPont Kevlar (2011)
  • Define cyberwar and cyberterrorism. → Cyberwar = disrupting a state/org for strategic/military purposes; cyberterrorism = hacking to conduct politically motivated terrorist activity
  • Describe the 2002 root DNS server attack and its significance. → A DDoS jammed 9 of 13 root DNS servers (peak 6% requests unanswered); quickly mitigated but demonstrated the potential scale of cyberterrorism
